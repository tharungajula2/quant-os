Imagine an architect building a house: they need to understand both design (banking rules) and engineering (machine learning).  In the same way, our quantitative risk architect straddles the worlds of banking and AI. Tharun, as an illustrative architect, brings together deep banking knowledge (credit portfolios, regulatory capital) and advanced data science (ML, AI) to build a “Credit Risk OS” – a unified model framework. This is akin to a translator fluent in two languages, connecting internal bank data with regulatory requirements and AI models for decision-making.

In practice, banks demand exactly this skillset.  Quant roles often involve integrating complex risk models (IFRS 9 expected loss, capital models) with new technologies (AI model validation, climate risk models).  For example, Deloitte describes quant analysts who “bring a rigorous, evidence-based analytical capability” to solve complex problems, including quantitative modeling for **climate risk stress testing** and **AI model validation**, as well as credit risk and IFRS 9 models.  In regulatory terms, Basel and regulatory guidelines expect such experts to align internal models with Pillar 1/2 rules, embed forward-looking analyses, and support **Model Governance (SR 11-7)** and capital planning.

Thus, Tharun’s “Credit Risk OS” is built to connect the entire network of concepts – from [[Probability-of-Default]] and [[Loss-Given-Default]] models up through IFRS 9 provisioning, [[Advanced-ML-in-Risk]] techniques like XGBoost and SHAP, and regulatory oversight in [[SR-11-7-Model-Governance]]. Each piece is integrated (like rooms in a house), ensuring that data pipelines, risk parameters and decisioning all work together under sound governance.
