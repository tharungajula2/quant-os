---
title: "Low-Default Portfolios (LDP)"
date: 2026-03-14
tags: [LDP, sovereign, bank, rating]
cluster: Specialized Portfolios & Stress
---

Some portfolios rarely see defaults – think of highly rated sovereigns or large financials. These are known as **Low-Default Portfolios (LDPs)**. Basel II doesn’t define LDPs explicitly, but recognizes that portfolios like sovereigns, banks or highly-rated corporates have few losses.  With sparse data, statistically estimating PD/LGD is tricky: you might observe zero defaults in a decade, but that doesn’t mean zero risk.

Regulators suggest that even for LDPs, banks use “long-run average” or external data to inform risk models. For example, if a country has never defaulted, a bank might use historical averages from similar countries or apply a supervisory PD floor. Indeed, Basel often imposes floors (e.g., minimum PD of 0.03%) for AAA exposures.  The Basel Newsletter notes that LDPs form a continuum and need supplemented data: pooling industry data or applying judgment is common.

Special cases exist: sovereign exposures in IRB can use CRAs or internal ratings with caution. For corporate LDPs (e.g. project finance in a small portfolio), banks might treat them as specialized lending or apply higher multipliers. Importantly, IFRS 9/ECL still requires forward-looking PD even for LDPs, possibly using macro scenarios to gauge risk.

In practice, LDP treatment ties to risk parameters: banks may set conservative LGDs or limit growth in LDP exposure. Supervisors monitor whether the lack of defaults has led to underestimation of risk. Ongoing challenges include validating these models (lack of backtest data) and satisfying [[SR-11-7-Model-Governance]]. When front-line models lack data, expert overlays or qualitative factors are often required by regulators.

In short, LDPs are portfolios with very few defaults (e.g. sovereigns, banks).  Estimating PD/LGD for LDPs demands careful use of long-term statistics or external benchmarks. The goal is to avoid complacency: even if a dataset is “low default”, the bank must still provision and capitalise for risk appropriately.
