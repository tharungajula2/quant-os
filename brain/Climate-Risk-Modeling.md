Climate risk is the new factor in the credit sky.  It has two faces: **physical risk** (damage from events like floods, storms, wildfires) and **transition risk** (economic shifts from moving to a low-carbon economy, like carbon pricing or regulations). Banks now assess how these affect credit.  For instance, a mortgage portfolio in coastal areas faces higher physical risk (sea-level rise), while an oil company loan faces transition risk (stranded assets as demand falls).

Regulators are formalizing this: the Basel Committee has voluntary climate disclosure frameworks. Banks must identify material climate risks (physical vs transition) and the time horizon (short/medium/long term).  Climate stress testing is emerging – banks run scenarios (e.g. IEA Net-Zero Path) to project credit losses. Academically, climate factors are being integrated into PD/LGD models.  For example, BIS research uses structural models to adjust PDs for temperature rise or policy changes. Another study uses integrated assessment models to estimate climate-driven default rates across a loan portfolio.

In practice, modeling climate risk means overlaying climate scenarios on top of credit risk models. For physical risk, lenders might factor in increased default probability for loans in high-risk zones. For transition risk, they might stress test borrowers with carbon-intensive activities (e.g. using higher PD and LGD). The Basel disclosure framework specifically requires banks to explain which risks they consider physical vs transition. Moreover, climate risk connects to [[Macro-Stress-Testing]]: central banks encourage including climate scenarios in ICAAP.

In brief, climate risk modeling is about adding new stress dimensions to existing credit models. It builds on foundation elements (PD, LGD, scenario analysis) but with forward-looking climate data.  Though still evolving, best practice involves scenario analysis and alignment with global standards (TCFD, NGFS). The result: banks start to report how a warming world might hurt their loan book, ensuring the “digital brain” of credit risk covers this cross-domain risk.
