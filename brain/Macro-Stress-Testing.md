Stress testing is like taking your ship into a storm in a simulator to see if it stays afloat. In banking, **macro stress testing** assesses how extreme economic scenarios (deep recession, market crashes) impact a bank’s capital.  In the US, the Federal Reserve’s CCAR (Comprehensive Capital Analysis and Review) and DFAST require large banks to run forward-looking, multi-year stress tests annually.  Similarly, EU banks conduct Pillar 2 ICAAP stress tests under regulatory scrutiny, often guided by EBA/ECB scenarios.

A stress test typically applies a hypothetical macro scenario (e.g. GDP drop, unemployment spike, interest rate shock) to credit risk, market risk and other models. For credit, PDs, LGDs and EADs are shocked to reflect deteriorated conditions. For example, default rates might double and LGDs rise in a recession. IFRS 9 ECL models also get stressed: regulators now ask firms to incorporate IFRS 9 scenarios into their ICAAP capital forecasts.  Banks then calculate stressed capital ratios, checking if they hold enough capital even after big losses.

Regulators have published principles for sound stress testing (e.g. BCBS 2012) emphasizing integration into risk management. Scenarios must be severe but plausible, covering multiple risk types.  CCAR is quantitative and qualitative: banks must have not only the numbers but also credible capital plans. Since 2020, the Fed’s stress capital buffer simplifies this by integrating stress results into overall capital requirements.  The bottom line: these regulatory stress tests ensure banks think beyond the average year and quantify capital needed to survive tail risks.

Modeling stress links back to IFRS 9 and credit components: a stressed scenario might increase *Point-in-Time* PDs and reduce collateral values (raising LGDs). In credit risk jargon, stress pushes expected losses up and forces unexpected losses into outcomes. Importantly, climate and new risks are being incorporated: e.g. regulators encourage scenario analysis of climate-related events in stress tests. In all, macro stress testing ties economic narrative to model parameters – amplifying [[Probability-of-Default]], [[Loss-Given-Default]] etc under stress – and ensures capital adequacy under extreme yet plausible events.
