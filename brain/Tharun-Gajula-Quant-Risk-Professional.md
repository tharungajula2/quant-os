## Narrative Foundation

The strongest way to understand this profile is as a progression from **financial intuition** to **regulatory rigor** to **technical execution**.

The financial intuition starts with the core mechanics of bank loss and capital. A bank lends money, estimates how often borrowers fail, estimates how much is lost when they fail, and then decides how much provision and capital are required to survive. That logic begins with [[Expected-vs-Unexpected-Loss.md]] and [[Economic-Capital-Basics.md]], then becomes more formal through [[Probability-of-Default.md]], [[Loss-Given-Default.md]], and [[Exposure-at-Default.md]].

The regulatory rigor enters when those parameters are no longer just academic ideas, but become part of official frameworks. [[Basel-IRB-Framework.md]] explains how internal risk estimates translate into prudential capital. [[RWA-Risk-Weighted-Assets.md]] explains how model outputs become regulatory balance-sheet pressure. [[IFRS-9-and-ECL.md]] explains how expected loss becomes accounting impairment. [[SR-11-7-Model-Governance.md]] explains why models must be independently challenged, monitored, and defended.

The technical execution layer is what makes the profile differentiated. It is one thing to understand Basel or IFRS 9 conceptually. It is another to build a scorecard, measure KS and AUC, monitor PSI drift, engineer WoE features, compare logistic regression with XGBoost, or explain a model using SHAP. That layer lives inside [[Weight-of-Evidence-and-IV.md]], [[Logistic-Regression-Scorecards.md]], [[Advanced-ML-in-Risk.md]], [[Model-Performance-Metrics.md]], and [[Population-Stability-Index-PSI.md]].

So the central story is this: the profile is being built not as a generic data scientist and not as a purely theoretical finance student, but as a **Quant Risk Architect** who can explain the business, defend the regulation, understand the model, and execute the pipeline.
