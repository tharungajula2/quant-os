Think of insurance: you set aside reserves for probable claims before they happen. IFRS 9 introduced a forward-looking “expected credit loss” (ECL) model, requiring banks to provision for credit losses from loan inception.  Unlike old rules (which waited for a loss), IFRS 9 tells firms to recognize expected losses based on current and future information.

Under IFRS 9, loans are classified into stages: **Stage 1** (no significant credit deterioration) triggers 12-month ECL; **Stage 2** (significant increase in credit risk) and **Stage 3** (credit-impaired) require *lifetime* ECL. Concretely, Stage 1 provisions cover the *expected loss in the next year*, while Stage 2/3 cover *expected loss over the loan’s remaining life*. The standard defines 12-month and lifetime ECL in terms of PD, LGD, and EAD:

> *“The ECL at each reporting date is the probability-weighted estimate of credit losses”*, summed over time.

For example, one summary explains that for 12-month ECL, banks use a one-year PD times LGD times EAD (discounted), whereas for lifetime ECL they use the probability of default over the remaining life as weights.  IFRS 9 therefore tightly links to [[Probability-of-Default]], [[Loss-Given-Default]], and [[Exposure-at-Default]]: those risk parameters drive the provision.

Practically, IFRS 9 often requires banks to overlay macroeconomic scenarios. Regulators expect boards to ensure ECL models incorporate multiple forecasts and judgment in Stage 2/3 assessments.  For stress testing and capital (ICAAP/CCAR), supervisors clarified banks should run stress ECL projections and may use transitional arrangements for capital to offset the jump in provisions.

In summary, IFRS 9 ECL replaces incurred-loss accounting with a three-stage, forward-looking provisioning framework. It distinguishes 12-month vs lifetime PD-based losses, and it requires scenario-based adjustments. This makes ECL provisioning closely aligned with IRB risk parameters (PD, LGD, EAD) and emphasizes early recognition of credit deterioration in capital planning and [[Macro-Stress-Testing]].
