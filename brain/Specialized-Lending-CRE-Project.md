Lending for large projects (like power plants or real estate developments) is unlike lending to a normal business. **Specialized lending** includes Commercial Real Estate (CRE) loans (e.g. office buildings, retail malls) and Project Finance (infrastructure projects). The key feature: repayment depends on the success of the project or collateral, not general cash flow. An analogy: funding a movie based only on its future ticket sales.

Under Basel IRB, specialized lending exposures fall into sub-classes: *Project Finance*, *Object Finance* (single asset leases), *Commodities Finance*, *Income-Producing Real Estate (IPRE)*, and *High-Volatility Commercial Real Estate (HVCRE)*. Each has unique risk traits. For example, project finance often has contingent features and equity buffers; it’s typically high-risk, requiring special risk weight formulas or supervisory parameters. CRE (non-HVCRE) may be less volatile but still tied to property markets. Regulators acknowledge that these portfolios often have few defaults historically but can suffer large losses when projects fail.

Capitalizing these requires care. Basel provides a **supervisory slotting approach** for many specialized loans: exposures are slotted into supervisory risk categories (Strong, Good, Satisfactory, Weak, Default) based on criteria (e.g. loan-to-cost, sponsor experience). Each category has an assigned risk weight (and implied LGD) in the rules. For example, a “Strong” project finance deal might get a lower risk weight (and LGD) than a “Weak” one. The slotting tables (BCBS CRE 33) define these weights explicitly. Institutions may use their own IRB PD/LGD for SL if approved, otherwise they rely on supervisory slotting metrics.

For CRE specifically (non-ADC mortgages), expected losses tend to follow general corporate behavior, but downturn LGDs can be high (property values drop). In stress testing (see [[Macro-Stress-Testing]]), CRE values are often shocked heavily. In practice, banks model CRE and project loans with specialized expert teams due to their complexity.

In summary, specialized lending like CRE and project finance has bespoke treatment under regulatory capital: either using strict slotting criteria or enhanced IRB calibration. It links back to [[Probability-of-Default]] and [[Loss-Given-Default]] but often requires higher conservatism (higher downturn LGDs, cautious PD). Banks monitor portfolio quality closely and may exclude new project finance lending if projected returns decline (especially under climate stress scenarios). Specialized lending highlights how credit risk models adapt to unique, large-scale exposures, ensuring capital covers these concentrated risks.
